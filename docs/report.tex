\section{Supplementary Analysis: Law Adoption and Homicide Rates Over Time}\label{sec:supp}
% ======================================================================

\par
To complement the Tableau visualizations, an event-study style chart
(Figure~\ref{fig:lawadoption}) was generated with AI based on a detailed
prompt to the Perplexity assistant. The goal was to compare how firearm
homicide rates evolve around the adoption of popular versus evidence-based
laws.

\begin{quote}
	\textbf{Prompt to Perplexity:}
	\textit{
		Using the Kang et al. firearm ownership
		proxy dataset and the Tufts CTSI State Firearm Law Database, generate a
		multi line event study showing the average firearm homicide rate from
		5 years before to 10 years after the adoption of 3 popular firearm laws
		versus 3 evidence based firearm laws.
		For the popular laws, use, Gun violence RO, Assault weapons ban, LCM Ban.
		For the evidence-based laws, use: permit to purchase, waiting period,
		universal BG check.
		Plot a separate line for each law, grouped visually into popular and
		evidence-based categories. Also include summary metrics comparing the
		average post-adoption homicide rates between the two groups at the
		bottom of the figure, and simplify the axis labels so they are shown
		only once for all charts. Only output the code needed to build this
		chart. (Prompt was better formatted, but \LaTeX doesn't let allow
		proper formatting in quote blocks).}
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
	\textbf{Response summary:} Produced a Plotly multi-line chart with a vertical
	reference line at year~0, solid colored lines for evidence-based laws and
	dashed lines for popular laws, grouped in a single shared axis system with
	common labels and a small table of average post-adoption homicide rates
	by law group beneath the chart.
\end{quote}

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{../figures/LawAdoptionChart.png}
	\caption{Supplementary -- Average Firearm Homicide Rate Around Law Adoption
		(Event Study, $-$5 to $+$10 years)}
	\label{fig:lawadoption}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Insights}:
\begin{itemize}
	\item All five laws are associated with a decline in firearm homicide rate
	      in the years following adoption, but the steepness of the decline
	      differs substantially by law type.
	\item Evidence-based laws show a noticeably steeper and more
	      sustained post-adoption decline than popular laws, consistent with the
	      findings from the Tableau bar chart
	      (Figure~\ref{fig:q3chart2}).
	\item Universal Background Checks shows the sharpest improvement, followed
	      by GVRO and Assault Restriction.
	\item Popular laws (Concealed Carry Permit and Waiting Period) show a modest
	      but slower improvement, reinforcing the conclusion that the type of law
	      matters more than its prevalence alone.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusions}
% ======================================================================

\subsection*{Main findings}

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Law strength and homicide rates}: States with fewer or weaker
	      firearm laws tend to have higher firearm homicide rates, while states
	      with stronger, more comprehensive laws sit at the lower end of the rate
	      distribution\sref{q1}.
	\item \textbf{Trends over time}: From the 1990s onward, groups of states with
	      more and stronger laws show larger and more sustained declines in
	      firearm homicide rates than groups with weaker law profiles\sref{q2}.
	\item \textbf{State-level patterns}: For many individual states, periods of
	      growth in firearm legislation align with improvements in their
	      homicide ranking relative to other states, even if the effect of any
	      single law is hard to isolate\sref{q3}.
	\item \textbf{Popular vs evidence-based laws}: The supplementary event-study
	      chart (Figure~\ref{fig:lawadoption}) suggests that evidence-based laws
	      are associated with steeper and more persistent post-adoption declines
	      in firearm homicide rates than more popular but weaker laws.
	\item \textbf{Role of ownership}: FSS is useful as a proxy for firearm
	      ownership, but the results indicate that policy design and enforcement
	      matter at least as much as the underlying level of gun prevalence.
\end{itemize}

\par
This project does not prove causality or control for every possible
confounder, but across multiple visualizations and groupings the same story
emerges: how a state chooses to regulate firearms is closely linked to how
often guns are used to kill.
